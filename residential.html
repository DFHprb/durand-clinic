Therapeutic Modality

Shinrin-yoku: Japanese Forest Bathing

Born in Japan in the 1980s and now backed by decades of clinical research, Shinrin-yoku — the practice of slow, guided immersion in forest environments — measurably lowers cortisol, reduces blood pressure, strengthens immune function, and quiets the nervous system.

On 100 acres of old-growth forest along the UNESCO Niagara Escarpment, guests experience guided forest bathing as part of their daily program. Not a walk. Not exercise. A deliberate, sensory practice designed to restore what chronic stress has eroded — and to do it in a setting that has been doing exactly this for 450 million years.
